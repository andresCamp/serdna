# serdna.dev v2 — Vision

## What This Is

serdna.dev is the portfolio site for Andrés Campos. It exists to convert two audiences into meetings:

1. **Immediate (10-day window):** Founders, CTOs, and hiring leads who need a product engineer to start now — contract or freelance.
2. **Bridge (30-60 days):** Engineering managers and recruiters at product-driven companies hiring full-time remote product engineers.

The site is not a resume. It is a controlled demonstration of capability, judgment, and taste. The site itself is the first case study.

A CTO visits serdna.dev and thinks: "This is a product engineer, not just a coder. I want them on my team."

## Positioning: Product Engineer as Builder

The market in 2026 is looking for product engineers who operate between PM and IC — people who shape the roadmap, talk to users, *and* ship code. The role is increasingly defined by ownership, not specialization.

Andrés already operates this way. Seven products across seven domains. Full-stack, AI-native, end-to-end. The positioning isn't aspirational — it's descriptive.

### What "Builder" Means Here

- Starts from the user experience, works backwards to the technology
- Ships production systems, not prototypes
- Makes hard architectural tradeoffs (agents vs. pipelines vs. stateless calls) based on the problem, not the trend
- Builds his own tools (Osis, Prompt Eval, Spectr) because the process matters as much as the product
- Has taste — opinions about craft, design, and how things should feel

### Market Context (April 2026)

Product engineer demand is strong across startups, SaaS, and AI-native companies. The bar is high: employers want immediate impact, product-sized thinking, and AI-aware execution. Most candidates are either pure engineers or pure product people. The hybrid who can do both — and has shipped to prove it — is the hire.

### Dual-Track Strategy

**Freelance (now):** The fastest path to cash is through existing connections and platforms (Upwork, direct outreach). The site needs to be credible enough to not disqualify, specific enough to qualify. The conversation does the rest.

**Full-time remote (bridge):** Once the immediate pressure is off, the site becomes the long game — recruiters, inbound, referrals. Same positioning, same pillars. The conversion just takes longer.

## The Four Pillars

Everything on the site proves one or more of these. If a section doesn't map to a pillar, it doesn't belong.

### 1. Builder with Taste — The Identity

Not just an engineer. A product engineer who cares about how things work *and* how they feel. The taste section makes this visible — four build principles, each backed by how Andrés applies them. The site's own craft is the meta-proof.

### 2. Unreasonably Effective — The Output

Seven products. Seven domains. Fourteen months. The work section speaks for itself. The systems section shows the depth — not just what was used, but why it was chosen and what was considered instead.

### 3. Judgment Over Hype — The Edge

The contrarian signal. Tried autonomous agents on MyStory, measured the latency, chose stateless LLM calls because the UX demanded it. In a market full of people reaching for agents for everything, the person who knows when an agent is wrong for the problem is rarer and more valuable.

### 4. Team Multiplier — The Hire-Me Signal

Led a 5-person dev team. Managed 20+ contributors. Taught a 12-week bootcamp. Runs a discovery-first process. Not a lone wolf. Someone who raises the ceiling for the people around them.

## Guardrails

### Do

- Lead with work, not credentials.
- Position as a builder with taste and judgment.
- Frame founder background as range and depth, not flight risk.
- Position dev tools as team-leverage.
- Show the thinking process through systems commentary and build principles.
- Keep copy direct, specific, confident.
- Lead contact with contract/freelance availability.
- The site itself must be evidence of craft.

### Don't

- Say "nontraditional path" — show it.
- Mention bridge jobs, survival mode, or financial pressure.
- Over-explain — let the work speak.
- Sound philosophical or visionary — wrong register.
- List every technology without context — show judgment about which tool for which problem.
- Use other people's quotes as taste proof — show your own principles and how you apply them.
